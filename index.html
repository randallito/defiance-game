⚠ A note on uncertainty

This game draws on empirical research about civil resistance, but what is happening in the United States is unprecedented in its speed. V-Dem's Democracy Report 2026 found the U.S. lost its liberal democracy status for the first time in over 50 years. M. Gessen warns this has moved beyond "democratic backsliding" into something qualitatively different. And Bianchi, Cheeseman, and Cyr (2025) find that since 1994, nearly 90% of democratic recoveries failed within five years. No model can reliably predict what works in a situation this novel.

Civil resistance movements are succeeding less often — but not primarily because autocrats have gotten smarter. Chenoweth's data shows nonviolent campaign success rates climbed as high as 65% in the 1990s but have fallen to less than 34% since 2010. The leading explanation is deteriorating movement quality: shorter campaigns, weaker organizational infrastructure, over-reliance on spectacle over sustained pressure. The strategic choices organizers make — capacity vs. spectacle, sustained coalitions vs. news cycles — are the primary determinants of success.

This game is a tool for sharpening strategic thinking and sparking conversation, not a map of the territory. It was built with AI assistance and may contain errors. It does not yet model AI-related factors (disinformation, surveillance, organizing tools), geopolitical dynamics (great power competition, global authoritarian coordination), or economic disruption (AI-driven unemployment, labor market precarity). These will be addressed in future iterations.
